INTRODUCTION

We are Team ASIMOV, Autonomous Site Identification Mapping and Observation Vehicle

“We are a group of 10 undergraduate students of SRM Institute of Science and Technology, Kattankulathur. Our group is devided into 5 domains namely Mechanical, Electrical, Life Sciences, Coding and Corporate. We are undertaking an ambitious project to develop an advanced vehicle capable of mapping the Black Canyon's terrain. Our objective is to identify optimal locations for soil and gas sample collection and conduct comprehensive rock analyses to categorize the geological diversity within the canyon. Upon completing the mapping and sampling, the vehicle will autonomously navigate to a pre-designated drop-off site to deposit the collected soil samples for further analysis. This project aims to deepen our understanding of the Black Canyon's geological landscape through precise, systematic exploration and sampling.”

ABOUT

“Our mission is divided into three segments. For the 1st segment, our vehicle maps the entire area of the Black Canyon region and sends all the data to the base station. We, at the base station, we then select a site suitable for sample collection and send the coordinated to the vehicle. For the 2nd segment, our vehicle follows the most ideal path to reach the chosen coordinates and starts digging to collect soil and gas samples. The samples are stored in a sterile container. Rock analysis is also performed at this stage. As a part of the 3rd segment, our vehicle moves to another site whose coordinates had been provided to the vehicle prior to the start of the 1st segment. Once it reaches the end point, it dumps out the collected sample for further testings and analysis.”

  • To traverse smoothly along the rocky terrain without damaging the vehicle, Traction wheels has been implemented to minimize slipping along with a Rocker-Bogie suspension system that will allow the vehicle to navigate challenging, rocky terrain with stability and ease.
  • To detect suitable soil for testing, During terrain mapping, the IR sensor pinpoints anomalous temperature regions that may suggest underlying geological features. It enables the vehicle to detect hot spots or cool areas, which might hint at subsurface activity, water content, or specific minerals worth investigating.
  • With the help of Webcams and IMU set-up on the vehicle, Visual features (like corners or edges) are extracted from the camera images to identify key landmarks in the environment. Using this data along with the fused visual and inertial data gathered by the algorithms, the system continuously estimates the camera's position and orientation, it then builds a 3D map of the environment.
  • Once the site for soil collection has been detected, the auger, surrounded by the casing is tasked to drill into the site. As it drills, the soil moves up the auger using the ridges and gets collected in the casing to ensure the soil doesn't fall off while rotating.
  • After detecting suitable site for rock analysis, the microscope is used to inspect the rock surface directly, identifying minerals, fossil-like structures, or textures that could reveal the rock's history or potential biological activity. The moisture sensor measures the moisture content in the soil, which is critical for evaluating past water activity or hydrated minerals.
  • Once the soil is collected to avoid contamination the casing serves as an opaque, sterile, and airtight container to safeguard soil samples from air and light exposure. Rubber sleeves and flaps provide an additional barrier, making it airtight, ensuring no interaction with the external environment post-collection.
  • We're using a lead screw to lower the auger, with a coupler allowing easy detachment of both the auger and casing for flexibility and compactness. Additionally, we're using Steel supports with weight reduction and an acrylic chassis to lower weight while maintaining rigidity and strength.
